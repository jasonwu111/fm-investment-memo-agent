# LP Investment Memo Template Schema v3

Status: Working schema calibrated against four approved investment recommendation memos  
Intended output language: English  
Primary audience: FM Investments / institutional LP investment professionals and decision makers  
Purpose: Define the recurring content, analytical coverage, and adaptable presentation of an LP investment recommendation memo without forcing every opportunity into one rigid outline.

## 1. Governing idea

This schema defines the investment questions that a memo should answer. It does not prescribe one mandatory sentence pattern, paragraph count, heading structure, or table design.

The four calibration memos show a stable underlying framework but meaningful differences in presentation. Accordingly:

- Required content means that the substance should be addressed when relevant and supportable.
- Required content does not always require a separate heading.
- Closely related modules may be combined when the result is clearer.
- A new fund should first be mapped to the recurring patterns in this schema.
- If the new fund does not fit those patterns, the writer may adapt the structure rather than omit material information or force an inaccurate comparison.
- Approved prior memos are calibration examples, not factual sources for a new fund.

The preferred outcome is a memo that is recognizable as an FM investment recommendation while remaining responsive to the strategy, legal structure, available evidence, and material underwriting issues of the specific opportunity.

## 2. LP perspective and drafting principles

- Write from the perspective of an institutional LP evaluating a GP, a specific fund or opportunity, and the legal vehicle through which FM may invest.
- Focus on facts and analysis material to an allocation decision.
- Distinguish among firm-level, strategy-level, current-fund, predecessor-fund, parallel-fund, related-vehicle, co-investment, and portfolio-company information.
- Preserve exact legal names, dates, currencies, percentages, thresholds, time periods, and calculation bases when they are material.
- Distinguish documented facts, GP representations, FM observations, calculations, and LP-side assessments when the distinction matters.
- Synthesize across sources rather than narrating documents one by one.
- Use neutral, concise, institutional-investment language. Avoid promotional phrasing and unsupported conclusions.
- Do not invent missing facts. Use transparent status language such as `TBD`, `Not disclosed`, `Not yet confirmed`, or `Awaiting discussion` when appropriate.
- Identify material conflicts or open diligence items for human review.
- Keep the target opportunity consistent throughout the memo and prevent cross-fund contamination.

## 3. The recurring memo spine

The following content families recur across the approved memos and should be the starting point for a new memo:

1. Document Title.
2. Core Investment Information.
3. Onsite Meetings and Subsequent Due Diligence.
4. Fund Terms Summary.
5. Investment Manager / Firm / Platform Context.
6. Team, Governance, and Decision-Making Bodies.
7. Key Persons.
8. Strategy.
9. Value Creation / Value-Add Capabilities.
10. Investment Process and Decision Making.
11. Material Changes to Strategy or Process, if applicable.
12. LP-Side Takeaways or Assessments, when useful and supportable.

This is a content map, not an inflexible table of contents. Sections 5 through 10 may be separated, combined, or reordered to fit the opportunity and the approved house style.

For example:

- `Firm` and `Team` may sit under an `Investment Manager` heading.
- Investment Committee information may appear in `Team`, in `Investment Process`, or in a dedicated governance subsection.
- Value-creation substance may appear under `Value Creation`, `Value-Add Capabilities`, `Strategy`, or `Investment Process`.
- A short process discussion may be integrated with Team / Investment Committee content.
- Strategy may include platform architecture, related-fund allocation, or market maps when those topics are central to understanding the opportunity.

## 4. Content priority levels

### 4.1 Core content

Core content should ordinarily appear in every investment recommendation memo:

- Document title.
- Core investment information.
- Fund terms.
- Firm or investment-manager context.
- Target-fund team and key decision makers.
- Strategy.

### 4.2 Expected content

Expected content should be covered when relevant information exists. It may be short, combined with another module, or presented in a different form:

- Due-diligence interaction history.
- Governance and Investment Committee structure.
- Value-creation or value-add model.
- Investment process and decision making.

If substantive information is unavailable, the writer should not create generic filler. A material absence may instead be identified as a diligence gap.

### 4.3 Conditional content

Conditional content should be included only when material to the opportunity:

- Summary of changes to strategy.
- Summary of process or governance changes.
- Related-fund or strategy-series explanation.
- Capital-allocation rules among related vehicles.
- Share-class or investor-class comparison.
- Prior-fund performance or attribution discussion.
- Management-fee discount analysis.
- LP-side takeaway callouts.
- Open-items or pending-diligence summary.

## 5. Document Title

### Preferred form

`FM INVESTMENTS – [SHORT FUND OR OPPORTUNITY NAME] – Investment Recommendation`

The title should clearly identify the recommendation being presented. A short commercial name may be used in the title even when the full legal fund and vehicle names appear in the core information and Fund Terms sections.

When the recommendation concerns linked funds or a primary fund plus an associated vehicle, the title should identify the principal opportunity and the memo should explain the relationship elsewhere.

## 6. Core Investment Information

### Common fields

Present the following near the beginning:

1. **Firm Name**
2. **Firm Point of Contact**, including email when available
3. **Investment Opportunity**
4. **Investment Vehicle Name**
5. **FM Global Investing Entity**
6. **Date**

### Interpretation

- `Firm Name` identifies the GP or investment manager.
- `Investment Opportunity` identifies the fund, strategy, or economic opportunity under review.
- `Investment Vehicle Name` identifies the legal entity in which FM would subscribe or invest.
- `FM Global Investing Entity` identifies the relevant FM investor.
- `Date` is the date of the investment recommendation.

### Variants and unresolved information

- Do not assume that the commercial fund name, legal fund name, GP entity, and FM investment vehicle are identical.
- If the legal vehicle is not yet established or confirmed, an approved memo may state `TBD` or another accurate status rather than inventing a name.
- If multiple funds or related vehicles are involved, identify the primary recommendation and explain the role of each related entity.
- If the source package contains multiple similarly named funds, confirm the target fund before extracting terms, team members, performance, or strategy information.

## 7. Onsite Meetings and Subsequent Due Diligence

### Purpose

Provide a concise chronological record of substantive FM interactions relevant to the opportunity through the memo date.

Despite the heading, the section may cover:

- Onsite or in-person meetings.
- Virtual meetings and calls.
- Fundraise launch sessions.
- Due-diligence webinars.
- Annual meetings and investor updates.
- Q&A sessions.
- Portfolio or co-investment reviews.
- Follow-up discussions and other material diligence touchpoints.

### Information to capture when available

- Date.
- Participants or relevant participant groups.
- Type or setting of interaction.
- Principal topic or purpose.
- Material update, observation, or follow-up, when useful.

### Presentation options

Choose the clearest format for the record:

- Separate dated entries with short labels.
- A compact chronological narrative.
- Short event paragraphs.
- A combination of the above.

No fixed verb or sentence pattern is required. The language should naturally reflect what occurred: a meeting, call, webinar, discussion, review, update, or other interaction.

Use named participants when they are important and reliably available. Broader descriptions such as `the FM team` may be appropriate when the approved record does not identify every attendee or the names are not decision-useful.

## 8. Fund Terms Summary

### Purpose and format

Fund Terms is a core section. A two-column `Term` / `Summary` table is generally preferred, but additional columns or small supplementary tables may be used when the economics vary by class, commitment size, closing date, or another condition.

The table should cover the material economics, duration, governance protections, borrowing provisions, and investor rights relevant to the opportunity. It should adapt to the strategy rather than mechanically reproduce a universal list.

### 8.1 Common term rows

Include the applicable items from the following recurring set:

- Fund Name.
- General Partner.
- Strategy / Stage Focus.
- Sector / Industry / Geography Focus.
- Target Fund Size and Hard Cap.
- Fund Currency or currency options.
- Closing Dates and fundraising progress.
- Base Management Fee.
- Management Fee after the investment period.
- Carried Interest.
- Hurdle Rate / Preferred Return.
- Investment Period.
- Fund Term and extensions.
- Waterfall / Catch-up / Carry Distribution.
- Fee Offset.
- Organizational Expenses or Additional Fees.
- Key Man / Key Person Provision.
- GP Commitment.
- Leverage / Subscription Facility.
- Recycling.
- LP Co-Investment.
- Fee Breaks / Discounts.
- Reasonableness of Fees.

### 8.2 Strategy- or structure-specific rows

Add rows when they are material to the particular fund, including, for example:

- Last Fund Size or predecessor-fund comparison.
- Diversification or concentration limits.
- Follow-on reserves.
- Ownership, stage, or check-size constraints.
- Parallel, feeder, sleeve, or alternative-vehicle mechanics.
- Capital allocation among related funds or strategies.
- Share classes or investor classes.
- Tiered carry based on performance or MOIC.
- Class-specific management fee, carry, waterfall, or other economics.
- Hedging.
- Liquidity, withdrawal, redemption, or transfer provisions, when applicable.
- Governance, LPAC, removal, no-fault, or other material investor protections.
- Insurance-, venture-, growth-, credit-, real-assets-, or other strategy-specific terms.

The examples above are not a closed list.

### 8.3 Drafting guidance

- Preserve fee bases, applicable periods, step-downs, thresholds, and exceptions.
- Distinguish target size, hard cap, current commitments, and predecessor-fund size.
- Keep the legal Key Man provision distinct from biographical Key Persons content.
- Summarize complex legal drafting accurately without copying unnecessary boilerplate.
- Where economics differ by class or commitment level, show the differences explicitly rather than blending them into one number.
- `Reasonableness of Fees` is an LP-side assessment. It may identify a term as in line with, above, or below relevant market practice only when supportable.
- Open terms may remain labeled `TBD`, `Not disclosed`, `Awaiting discussion`, or equivalent. Material unresolved terms should not be presented as final.
- If a material term is absent from the source package, flag the gap rather than borrowing the term from a predecessor or calibration memo.

## 9. Investment Manager / Firm / Platform Context

### Purpose

Give the reader enough organizational context to understand the GP, the target strategy's place within the broader platform, and why the platform matters to underwriting.

### Common subjects

Select the items that are most relevant:

- Founding, history, ownership, and leadership.
- AUM, personnel, offices, and geographic footprint, preferably with an as-of date when material.
- Main platforms, asset classes, strategies, or fund series.
- Relevant investment experience and market position.
- The target strategy's relationship to the broader firm.
- Shared resources, infrastructure, sourcing networks, or operating capabilities.
- Material organizational, governance, strategic, or performance-related evolution.
- The distinction between dedicated target-fund resources and broader firm resources.

### Related strategies and multi-fund platforms

Some opportunities cannot be understood in isolation. When material, explain:

- Adjacent strategies or related fund series.
- Differences in stage, ownership, check size, geography, or mandate.
- How opportunities are assigned among funds.
- Shared personnel, committees, sourcing systems, or support resources.
- Potential overlap, conflicts, boundary cases, or allocation risks.

A short comparison table may be useful. Do not provide an exhaustive platform catalogue unless it affects the investment case.

## 10. Team, Governance, and Decision-Making Bodies

### Purpose

Explain who is responsible for executing the strategy, how responsibilities are divided, and how important decisions are governed.

### Common team coverage

- Dedicated team size and composition.
- Senior leadership and day-to-day responsibility.
- Experience, tenure, continuity, and succession.
- Geographic, sector, stage, or functional coverage.
- Staffing model and deal-team construction.
- Access to broader firm resources.
- Operating, portfolio, capital-markets, recruiting, data, legal, compliance, finance, or other specialist support.
- Material hires, promotions, departures, reorganizations, or capacity considerations.
- Alignment, incentives, and accountability when material.

### Governance coverage

- Investment Committee, Growth Committee, Portfolio Review Committee, or other relevant bodies.
- Committee composition and permanent, rotating, or strategy-specific seats.
- Approval stages and decision rights.
- Voting requirements, veto rights, quorum, escalation, or challenge mechanisms.
- Allocation decisions among funds or vehicles.
- Portfolio monitoring and exit / hold governance.

### Flexible placement

Team and governance may be:

- Separate sections.
- Combined under `Team`.
- Included under an `Investment Manager` umbrella.
- Split between Team and Investment Process.

Avoid duplicating the same committee description in multiple places. The substance matters more than the heading.

## 11. Key Persons

### Purpose

Identify the individuals most relevant to leadership, sourcing, investment decisions, portfolio oversight, sector or geographic coverage, and value creation for the target strategy.

### Selection

Select people based on their actual relevance to the fund, not firm-wide seniority alone. Distinguish among:

- Target-fund leadership.
- Investment Committee or other decision makers.
- Sector or geographic leads.
- Operating or functional leaders.
- Legal Key Persons under the fund documents.

These groups may overlap but should not be assumed to be identical.

### Possible content

For each selected person, use the relevant subset of:

- Current title and role.
- Responsibility in the target strategy or governance process.
- Join year, tenure, age, or years of experience, when material and verified.
- Prior investment or operating roles.
- Sector, stage, functional, or geographic expertise.
- Representative investments or responsibilities.
- Education or qualifications, when useful.

### Presentation options

- Short prose biographies.
- A leadership or senior-team table.
- An organizational chart or team graphic accompanied by explanatory prose.
- A combination of selected biographies and a broader roster table.

Table columns should fit the evidence and purpose. Possible columns include `Name`, `Title`, `Role`, `Tenure`, `Experience`, `Prior Experience`, `Sector`, and `Education`; no single column set is mandatory.

## 12. Strategy

### Purpose

Explain what the fund intends to invest in, why the opportunity set may be attractive, how the strategy is expected to generate returns, and what the principal LP considerations are.

### Recurring strategy dimensions

Cover the dimensions that are material and supported:

- Investment stage and transaction type.
- Control, minority, structured, or other ownership orientation.
- Target company size, enterprise value, EBITDA, revenue, or equity check.
- Geography.
- Sector and subsector focus.
- Portfolio construction, target number of investments, pace, reserves, and concentration.
- Target-company characteristics and investment criteria.
- Sourcing model and claimed access advantage.
- Market opportunity and competitive context.
- Underwriting approach and expected return drivers.
- Use of co-investment.
- Return objectives, clearly distinguishing gross from net and fund-level from investment-level measures.
- Key execution, market, concentration, allocation, or strategy risks.

### Strategy presentation patterns

Use the pattern that best explains the actual fund:

- A concise overview followed by sector, geography, or investment-type bullets.
- A market map or thematic roadmap.
- A comparison of related fund series or adjacent strategies.
- A description of a platform or full-stack investment model.
- Target-company criteria or an investment-screen framework.
- A narrative explaining a specialized vertical or complex transaction approach.

Historical examples may illustrate the strategy but should not substitute for describing the current fund. Avoid assuming that every buyout, venture, growth, insurance, or other specialized fund should use the same strategy subsections.

## 13. Value Creation / Value-Add Capabilities

### Coverage expectation

The memo should ordinarily explain how the GP expects to support portfolio companies or otherwise create value. This substance is expected, but a standalone `Value Creation` heading is not mandatory.

It may appear under:

- `Value Creation`.
- `Value-Add Capabilities`.
- `Strategy`.
- `Investment Process`.
- Another clearly related heading.

### Possible coverage

- Overall ownership or engagement model.
- Timing of involvement from sourcing and diligence through ownership and exit.
- Operating partners, advisers, specialists, platform resources, or portfolio-support teams.
- Growth, pricing, sales-force effectiveness, product development, or market expansion.
- Cost optimization, procurement, working capital, or margin improvement.
- Technology, digital transformation, data, cybersecurity, or systems implementation.
- Talent assessment, recruiting, management change, and incentive design.
- Strategic repositioning.
- Add-on acquisitions, integration, and corporate development.
- Capital structure, financing, or deleveraging.
- How initiatives are developed, owned, measured, and monitored.
- Selected portfolio examples or dated evidence.
- Execution dependencies, attribution limitations, or unsuccessful examples.

Do not treat the existence of a large platform or operating team as proof of value creation. Distinguish GP claims, observed capabilities, and demonstrated results.

## 14. Investment Process and Decision Making

### Coverage expectation

Explain the actual process and controls that are most important to understanding how the strategy is executed. The section may be chronological, thematic, governance-focused, or organized by underwriting workstream.

### Possible chronological framework

- Sourcing.
- Initial screening and strategy fit.
- Preliminary approval.
- Due diligence.
- Investment Committee review.
- Final approval and signing.
- Post-close planning.
- Portfolio monitoring and exit / hold review.

### Possible underwriting-workstream framework

- Market definition and segmentation.
- Competitive position and market dynamics.
- Management assessment.
- Revenue, product, customer, and unit-economics analysis.
- Cost, operations, technology, data, and organizational assessment.
- Balance-sheet, capital-structure, downside, and scenario analysis.
- Legal, regulatory, insurance, or other strategy-specific diligence.
- Development and validation of the value-creation plan.

### Possible governance and allocation framework

- Deal staffing and leadership.
- Committee structure.
- Voting mechanics and decision rights.
- Independent review or challenge mechanisms.
- Capital allocation among related funds or vehicles.
- Treatment of boundary cases and conflicts.

Use only the frameworks relevant to the opportunity. Focus on concrete processes, decision rights, and controls rather than generic claims of `rigorous diligence`.

## 15. Material Changes to Strategy or Process

### Section status

Conditional. Use one or two separate sections only when a meaningful change is supported and relevant to the LP decision.

### Strategy changes may include

- Sector additions, removals, or de-emphasis.
- Geographic expansion or contraction.
- Changes in stage, ownership, company size, or complexity orientation.
- Changes in portfolio construction, concentration, reserves, or co-investment.
- New investment screens or lessons from prior investments.
- Changes in value-creation emphasis, leverage, or return objectives.

### Process changes may include

- Committee restructuring.
- New rotating members, voting rules, independent review, or challenge mechanisms.
- New downside-case or assumption-review requirements.
- Changes to approval stages or decision rights.
- Earlier involvement by operating or specialist resources.
- New post-close planning, portfolio review, or exit processes.

### Recommended analytical form

When included, explain:

1. The prior approach.
2. The current approach.
3. Why the change occurred.
4. Evidence that it is being implemented.
5. The likely LP implication, including remaining execution risk.

If no meaningful change is supported, omit the section. Do not manufacture a change narrative merely because predecessor funds exist.

## 16. LP-Side Takeaways and Assessments

### Purpose

Approved memos may include concise LP-side conclusions within or after major sections. These can help translate description into decision-useful analysis.

Possible formats include:

- A bold `Takeaway` sentence.
- A short concluding paragraph.
- A reasonableness assessment in the Fund Terms table.
- A balanced observation embedded in the narrative.

Takeaways are optional and should be used selectively. They should:

- Follow from cited or traceable evidence.
- State why the issue matters to an LP.
- Distinguish strengths from dependencies or risks.
- Avoid becoming an unsupported recommendation or marketing statement.

Not every section needs a takeaway.

## 17. Presentation patterns validated by the calibration memos

When a new opportunity resembles one of the following patterns, the writer should generally prefer the corresponding presentation. These are defaults, not mandatory templates.

### Pattern A: Traditional sector-based buyout fund

Useful presentation:

- Firm and Team narrative.
- Selected Key Person biographies.
- Strategy overview followed by sector, geography, check-size, and portfolio-construction detail.
- Standalone Value Creation and Investment Process sections when sufficient material exists.
- Separate discussion of material strategy or process changes.

### Pattern B: Broad platform with a dedicated regional or market segment team

Useful presentation:

- Firm overview distinguishing the broader organization from the dedicated target-fund team.
- Team scale, local offices, broader platform resources, and governance.
- Key Person biographies and, if useful, a leadership table.
- Strategy framed around the target market, sourcing advantage, complexity, and repeatable value-add capabilities.

### Pattern C: Venture or growth strategy within a multi-strategy platform

Useful presentation:

- Explanation of the strategy series and the target fund's role within it.
- Related-fund comparison or allocation mechanics.
- Team / committee structure, voting mechanics, and boundary cases.
- Strategy expressed through market maps, investment roadmaps, platform models, or stage-specific criteria.
- Selective LP takeaway callouts.

### Pattern D: Specialized vertical strategy within a large alternative-investment firm

Useful presentation:

- Firm context followed by the dedicated vertical team and its access to broader resources.
- Class-specific or option-specific economics where applicable.
- Selected biographies plus a broader senior-team table.
- Investment Process organized by industry-specific underwriting and value-creation workstreams.

### Use of the pattern library

- Select the closest pattern only after reviewing the actual sources.
- Combine patterns when the opportunity genuinely spans more than one.
- Add a new pattern when material features do not fit the existing four.
- Never infer facts merely because the opportunity resembles a calibration pattern.

## 18. Adaptive outline selection

Before drafting, choose an outline based on the evidence and the investment question.

### Compact outline

Use when the opportunity and available evidence are relatively straightforward:

1. Title and Core Information.
2. Due-Diligence History.
3. Fund Terms.
4. Investment Manager: Firm, Team, and Key Persons.
5. Strategy and Value Creation.
6. Investment Process, if material.
7. Material Changes or Takeaways, if applicable.

### Expanded outline

Use when the team, strategy, governance, or platform relationships require separate treatment:

1. Title and Core Information.
2. Due-Diligence History.
3. Fund Terms.
4. Firm / Platform.
5. Team.
6. Governance / Investment Committee.
7. Key Persons.
8. Strategy.
9. Value Creation.
10. Investment Process.
11. Strategy or Process Changes.
12. Selected LP Takeaways.

### Specialized or multi-fund outline

Use when related strategies, vehicles, classes, or allocation rules are central:

1. Title and Core Information.
2. Opportunity and Related-Vehicle Context.
3. Due-Diligence History.
4. Fund Terms, including class or vehicle comparison.
5. Firm / Platform and Strategy Series.
6. Dedicated Team and Governance.
7. Key Persons.
8. Target Strategy and Market Map.
9. Allocation, Voting, or Boundary-Case Mechanics.
10. Value-Add and Investment Process.
11. Material LP Takeaways and Open Items.

## 19. Evidence mapping for a new memo

Before writing final prose, map available evidence to the recurring content families.

### Step 1: Lock the target identity

Confirm:

- Firm.
- Target fund or opportunity.
- Legal investment vehicle.
- Strategy and vintage.
- Relevant predecessor and related vehicles.

### Step 2: Classify the opportunity

Identify the closest presentation pattern and the features that require deviation.

### Step 3: Build a section map

For each potential content module, record:

- Whether it is relevant.
- Whether supporting evidence exists.
- The strongest source or sources.
- Material conflicts or open items.
- Whether the module should be standalone or combined.

### Step 4: Select the outline

Choose the compact, expanded, specialized, or another evidence-driven outline. Do not create empty headings.

### Step 5: Draft and synthesize

Use the approved memo patterns for tone and organization, but derive every fund-specific statement from the current opportunity's sources.

### Step 6: Validate

Confirm that:

- The memo consistently refers to the correct fund and vehicle.
- Fund terms are not taken from another fund or class.
- Team members and governance bodies are relevant to the target strategy.
- Historical examples are clearly identified as historical.
- Required substance is covered even when headings are combined.
- Unresolved items remain visibly unresolved.

## 20. Evidence record

Maintain a practical review trail for material facts and conclusions. The format may vary, but it should ordinarily capture:

- Memo section or field.
- Extracted fact or conclusion.
- Source document and useful locator.
- Source date or version, when relevant.
- Classification as documented fact, GP representation, FM observation, calculation, or LP-side interpretation.
- Material conflict, uncertainty, or follow-up need.

The clean memo need not display citations unless required by the house format. The evidence record supports review and correction; it should not overwhelm the final memo.

## 21. Handling incomplete, conflicting, or excessive information

### Incomplete information

- Draft only what is supported.
- Use an accurate status label for unresolved facts.
- Identify material gaps requiring follow-up.
- Do not fill gaps from an unrelated or predecessor fund unless the text explicitly identifies the information as historical context.

### Conflicting information

- Prefer final legal or authoritative current-fund materials for legal terms.
- Consider document date, version, scope, and authority.
- Do not silently choose between material conflicts.
- Flag conflicts that could affect the investment decision.

### Excessive information

- Prioritize decision-relevant facts.
- Use tables, bullets, and subheadings where they improve comprehension.
- Avoid repeating the same organizational or process description across Firm, Team, Strategy, and Investment Process.
- Move background detail out of the main narrative when it does not affect underwriting.

## 22. Style and final quality review

- Use professional, concise, analytical English.
- Prefer synthesis over source-by-source narration.
- Use headings and formats that fit the content; do not force visual uniformity at the expense of clarity.
- Preserve meaningful uncertainty.
- Avoid unsupported superlatives and GP marketing language.
- Keep the tone balanced: describe strengths, evidence, dependencies, and risks.
- Use examples selectively and identify their period or fund when relevant.
- Check all names, dates, currencies, percentages, ranges, roles, fee bases, and legal entities.
- Confirm that tables and narrative agree.
- Confirm that the memo reads as one coherent underwriting document rather than a collection of extracted passages.

## 24. Relationship to drafting rules

This schema answers: **What should the memo cover, and how may that coverage be organized?**

Separate section-by-section drafting rules may address source prioritization, evidence retrieval, conflict resolution, analytical framing, length, and quality assurance. Those rules should apply this schema with judgment and should not turn the memo into a rigid sentence generator.
